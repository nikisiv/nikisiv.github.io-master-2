Present

The 3D printing technology is growing by leaps and bounds and a million personal 3D printers are expected to be sold by the year 2020. There are multiple repositories available online that provides open-source blueprints for 3D fabrication. Websites like Thingverse allow 3D printing of replacement parts for automobiles and the likes. It has become very common for people to get these automobile parts printed for actual use and at a comparatively lesser cost.

Currently, 3D printing has started facilitating a new age of personal fabrication where people can get their digital designs 3D printed to form actual prototypes at a low cost. This greatly helps at-home inventors and designers. This has resulted in an easy entry into the manufacturing business without a great deal of investment in stocking or tooling. Websites like Shapeways and i.materialise allow people that upload 3D models to open an online store and sell products.

In the year 1989, Scott Crump invented the Fused Deposit Modeling technique. This technique has a string of solid material called the filament, which is heated by a nozzle. The filament melts and can be extruded on a path created by the computer. Once one layer is extruded, it cools down and solidifies. The subsequent layers are laid on top of each other to create the 3D structure. This is the most common technology used for desktop 3D printing and is most suitable for quick, low-cost prototyping.

Currently, 3D cloud printing is gaining prominence where digital files uploaded on cloud can be 3D printed anywhere around the world. This has removed the geographical constraints for businesses and can also eradicate shipping or transportation costs that usually arise when resources are shipped across international borders.
